# Three-Phase Blueprint for Increasing Vertical Jump and Dunking

> **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JVQW9H7gc0o
> **Date:** 2026-06-01

## Core Principles

- Genetics influences jump height, but improvement is always within your control regardless of starting point
- The body adapts only as much as the environment demands — variety in training prevents plateaus
- Strength and speed of force production are both required; neither alone is sufficient
- The only true failure is quitting; long plateaus are normal even at elite levels
- Shorter athletes must be proportionally stronger than taller athletes to achieve equivalent jump heights

---

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### The Three Phases

**Phase 1 — Build the Engine (Strength)**
- Goal: produce maximum force
- Two sub-phases:
  - **Hypertrophy**: grow the muscle
  - **Intensity**: train the nervous system to recruit those muscles more effectively
- Target muscles: hips, hamstrings, calves, quads
- Lift 2–3x per week
- Avoid **block periodisation** (all strength → then all jumping) — it is the worst periodisation method; variety is superior

**Phase 2 — Build the Springs (Tendons & Nervous System)**
- Train the **stretch-shortening cycle**: fast eccentric → fast concentric → amplified force output
- Tendons store and release energy; forces in jumping can exceed 10x bodyweight
- Start jumping **once per week**, increase volume by ~10% per week
- Build toward 2 sessions/week for most adults; young athletes may handle 3–4 sessions/week
- Must be built up gradually over months — skipping this causes injury (e.g., chronic knee pain)

**Phase 3 — Supercharge: Bridge Strength to Speed (The Hack)**
- Problem: squats take seconds; jumping ground contact is **~0.2 seconds** (one-foot) or up to 0.3 seconds (two-foot)
- Being strong but slow = leaving inches on the table
- Solution: **Olympic lifts**, specifically **power cleans**
  - Ground contact time mirrors the double knee bend timing
  - Motor pattern of first pull → double knee bend mirrors penultimate step → plant
  - Heavy catch overloads the eccentric phase uniquely
  - In a study of 1,000+ athletes (Satiar Ferogi), the power clean had the highest correlation to vertical jump of any weight room exercise

### Force-Velocity Curve
- Heavier load = slower movement speed
- Exercises can be mapped across a load-velocity spectrum
- Distributing training across this curve bridges the gap between max strength and explosive jumping

### Vertical Jump Deficit Training
- Three distinct athlete profiles:
  1. Strong but slow force production (velocity deficit)
  2. Fast but weak (strength deficit)
  3. Poor jump technique
- Identify and address your specific weakness first

### Long-Term Development Timeline
- Squat PRs precede athletic peak by ~5 years on average (per Mike Young's case study compilation)
- Power clean PRs precede athletic peak by ~1–2 years
- Training consistently builds tendon stiffness, neural efficiency, and muscle mass even when PRs are not happening

---

## Actionable Advice

- **Get brutally strong** in hips, hamstrings, calves, and quads — no one who jumps regularly has ever been "too strong"
- **Lower your rim** and practise max-effort approach dunks regularly; the intent of trying to dunk overloads the stretch-shortening cycle more effectively than generic plyometrics
- **Learn the power clean** — teachable to a complete beginner in ~20 minutes; do not skip it
- Build jump volume incrementally: start at 1 session/week, add ~10% volume weekly
- If you're 5'9" or shorter, benchmark your strength standards higher than taller athletes you compare yourself to
- Lift 2–3x/week while building jump sessions in parallel — do not separate the two into distinct blocks
- Diagnose whether your deficit is strength, velocity, or technique — train the weakest link first
- When on a plateau, stay consistent; PRs frequently emerge after extended stagnant periods when conditions align (health, low fatigue, high adrenaline)

---

## Things to Avoid

- **Block periodisation**: separating strength training and jumping into sequential isolated phases — leads to plateaus and wasted adaptation
- **Jumping every day without a strength base** — led to chronic knee pain (5 years of issues in the author's case); forces in jumping exceed 10x bodyweight and require structural preparation
- **Ignoring force velocity** — training only max strength without bridging to explosive, fast movements leaves vertical gains unrealised
- **Quitting during plateaus** — a 3-year vertical plateau preceded a world record performance; stagnation is a normal part of the process, not a signal to stop

---

## Key Takeaways

- Vertical jump improvement requires three integrated elements: **maximum strength**, **tendon/nervous system reactivity**, and **explosive power bridging** via Olympic lifts — training only one is insufficient
- The **power clean** is the single most correlated weight room exercise to vertical jump performance and should not be omitted
- **Force-velocity specificity** matters: a squat takes seconds; a jump ground contact is 0.2s — training must include exercises that develop rapid force production
- Short athletes must achieve **higher relative strength** than tall athletes to reach equivalent jump performance
- Long-term consistency beats short-term optimisation — elite athletes typically PR their vertical **5 years after their squat PR**; plateaus are expected, not failures
